Buser-Sarnak construction

Contents

  • References

This is a construction of an explcit Riemann surface that holds the record for having the largest smallest closed geodesic. This sort of a construction is called a Shimura curve. It is mentioned in [1].

Here is how it works. Take \(D = (a,b; \mathbb{Q})\), a quaternion division algebra. We want the division algebra to be split at infinity, that is \[\begin{equation} D \otimes \mathbb{R} = M_2(\mathbb{R}), \end{equation}\] where the right hand side is the ring of \(2 \times 2\) matrices over the reals. To have that \(D\) is a division algebra over \(\mathbb{Q}\), we demand that \[\begin{equation} x_0^{2} - ax_1^{2} - b x_2^{2} + ab x_3^{2} = 0 \end{equation}\] has no solutions for \(x_0,x_1,x_2,x_3 \in \mathbb{Q}\) but of course we admit solutions over \(\mathbb{R}\). This gives us an isomorphism \[\begin{equation} \left(D \otimes \mathbb{R}\right)^{(1)} \simeq SL_2(\mathbb{R}). \end{equation}\]

Fix a prime \(p\). The set of elements of the form \[\begin{align} \Gamma & = \{ x_0+ x_1 i + x_2 j + x_3 k , \ x_0,x_1,x_2,x_3 \in \mathbb{Z} \} \cap \left( D \otimes \mathbb{R} \right)^{(1)}, \\ \Gamma(p) & = \{ x_0+ x_1 i + x_2 j + x_3 k \in \Gamma, p\mid x_1,x_2,x_3 \}, \end{align}\] form a discrete subgroups of \(SL_2(\mathbb{R})\). The space \(\Gamma(p) \backslash \mathbb{H}\) is the one with a large systole.
