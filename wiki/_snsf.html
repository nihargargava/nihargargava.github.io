What are lattices?

Consider \(\mathbb{R}^d\) with the standard inner product. A lattice \(\Lambda\subseteq \mathbb{R}^d\) is a discrete subgroup such that the quotient space \(\mathbb{R}^d / \Lambda\) has a finite induced volume which we henceforth will call the covolume of \(\Lambda\). Throughout this article, we only mean lattices to denote these Euclidean lattices.

A more simple but equivalent idea of a lattice is that it is a set \[\begin{equation} \Lambda = \mathbb{Z}v_1 + \mathbb{Z} v_2 + \dots + \mathbb{Z} v_d, \end{equation}\] for some \(d\) vectors \(v_1,v_2,\dots,v_d \in \mathbb{R}^d\). If we were to write instead take \(\Lambda' = \mathbb{Z} w_1 + \dots + \mathbb{Z} w_d\) for some other basis of vectors \(w_1, \dots, w_d \in \mathbb{R}^{d}\), we must get the relation that \(\Lambda' = \Lambda\) will be the same lattice if and only if \[\begin{equation} w_i = \sum_{j=1}^{d}a_{ij} v_j, \text{ for some } a_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z} \end{equation}\] and the \(a_{ij}\) form a matrix whose inverse also has integer entries. Such a matrix \([a_{ij}]\) must have determinant \(\pm 1\). The covolume of the lattice \(\Lambda\) in this point of view is the volume of a \(d\)-dimensional parallelepiped that the vectors \(v_1,\dots,v_d\) span, or in other words the absolute value of a matrix whose columns are \(v_1\dots v_d\).

Lattices are fascinating objects with connections to number theory, physics, Lattice arrangements connect with many areas in mathematics such as string theory, harmonic analysis, hyperbolic geometry, finite groups, error-correcting codes and some very beautiful areas of number theory like elliptic curves, modular forms, finite fields, etc [1].

Post quantum cryptography and lattices

Lattice-based cryptography is an emerging area that relies on the computational difficulty in finding short vectors in a lattice. In most lattice-based encryption protocols, if an unauthorized adversary is able to decipher the encrypted messages, they can do so only with the aid of an efficient algorithm to find short lattice vectors which whose existence is known to be near-impossible (NP-Hard).

Homogeneous spaces

\(\DeclareMathOperator{\SL}{SL}\) Let \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})\) denote the group of unimodular square matrices of size \(d\). Then the equivalence of the two definitions of lattices mentioned above imply that the following is a surjective map onto unit covolume lattices in \(\mathbb{R}^{d}\). \[\begin{equation} g \mapsto g \mathbb{Z}^{d} , \ g \in \SL_d(\mathbb{R}). \end{equation}\] This informs us that the space of unit covolume lattices is in bijection with \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})/\SL_d(\mathbb{Z})\). This has the structure of a smooth \((d^{2}-1)\)-dimensional manifold. On this space, there is a canonical description of a measure due to the general theory of Haar measure on locally compact groups (see [2]). What this means is that there is a natural way to compute volumes of reasonably defined subsets \(A \subseteq \SL_d(\mathbb{R})/\SL_d(\mathbb{Z})\). What is remarkable about this construction is that even though it is not compact, the amount of space inside \(\SL(\mathbb{R})/\SL(\mathbb{Z})\) is finite. Hence it is a probability space.

This space \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})/\SL_d(\mathbb{Z})\) is a standard example of what is called a homogeneous space. Loosely put, a homogeneous space is a space with a continuous group of symmetries, in this case \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})\). Homogeneous spaces are related to lots of fascinating areas in mathematics and physics, and continue to bridge breathtaking connections in geometry, number theory and dynamics.

Division algebras

A seemingly unrelated rings that have all the axioms of a field except that it may not be commutative. A division alebra over a field \(k\) is a finite dimensional vector space over \(k\).

William Hamilton was the first person to discover a division algebra over \(\mathbb{R}\) which is the only non-commutative one over \(\mathbb{R}\) (apart from \(\mathbb{C}\) and \(\mathbb{R}\) itself, which are commutative). Over \(\mathbb{Q}\), there are infinitely many non-commutative division algebras, apart from just the number fields which are commutative. All such division algebras are known to be cylic division algebras.

Random lattices with arithmetic structure

Given a lattice \(\Lambda\), choose \(r= r_{\text{pack}}\) and consider the open balls \(\{ B_{r}\left( v \right)\}_{v \in \Lambda}\), which implies that for any \(v_1, v_2 \in \Lambda\), \(B_{r}(v_1) \cap B_{r}(v_2) \neq \emptyset \Rightarrow v_1 = v_2\). Here \(r_{\text{pack}}\) is the packing radius of the lattice, i.e., half of the length of the shortest non-zero vector.

This setup of spheres is called a lattice sphere packing, or simply lattice packing inside \((\mathbb{R}^d, \langle \ , \ \rangle)\). Packing density of a lattice packing is \[\begin{equation} \Delta(\Lambda) = \lim_{R \rightarrow \infty} \frac{\mu\left( B_{R}(0) \cap \left( \bigsqcup_{v \in \Lambda} B_{r}(v) \right) \right)}{\mu\left( B_{R}(0) \right)} = \frac{\mu( B_{r_{\text{pack}}}(0) )}{ \mu(\mathbb{R}^d / \Lambda)}. \end{equation}\]

If we denote \(\SL(V)\) to be the group of all unimodular linear transformations on \(V\), we can now define \[\begin{align} c_{d} = \sup\left\{ \mu\left( g B_{r}(0)\right) \ | \ r> 0,~g \in \SL(V) \text{ and } g B_{r}(0) \cap \Lambda_0 = \{ 0\}\right\}. \end{align}\] This quantity \(c_d\) is related to the definition in by \[\begin{equation} \tfrac{1}{2^{d}} c_d = \sup_{\substack{\Lambda \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{d},\\ \Lambda \text{ a lattice}} } \Delta(\Lambda). \end{equation}\] It then follows that for any lattice \(\Lambda \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{d}\), \(\Delta(\Lambda) \ge \frac{1}{2^{d}} c_d\). Intended project in Paris

This research builds upon recent advancements in the equidistribution of closed geodesics on compact hyperbolic surfaces, as presented by Dr. Nguyen-Thi Dang (Orsay) and Dr. Jialun Li (Polytechnique) in a recent preprint [3]. There, they obtained a higher rank version of the Bowen-Margulis equidistribution theorem, also sometimes known as the prime geodesic theorem.

Let us try to explain the Bowen-Margulis theorem in a simpler setting.

Their research extends the theorem by demonstrating the equidistribution of flat tori in the quotient space \(\Gamma \backslash G / M\), where \(G\) is a higher rank semisimple Lie group without compact factors, \(\Gamma\) is a cocompact subgroup, and \(M\) is the compact part of the centralizer of the diagonal group \(A\) and \(\Gamma\) is a torsion free discrete subgroup.

During a recent presentation by Dr. Nguyen-Thi Dang at the École de Printemps en Géométrie et Dynamique 2023 in Lille, a question was posed regarding the statistical behavior of the module of flat tori as the volume of the tori or the systole goes to infinity. Along with Dr. Dang, Dr. Li, and Assistant Prof. Samuel Lelièvre (Orsay), I am currently conducting research to address this question. We are investigating simpler cases through numerical experiments and simulations. Our focus is on the equidistribution of unit lattices in number fields, a topic of interest due to conjectures attributed to Gromov and Margulis [4]. Our simulations are being conducted on simple setups, such as towers of two quadratic fields, as the systole or covolume of the lattice goes to infinity.
