Brauer group

Contents

  • Examples
  • Galois cohomology

For a field \(k\), the Braur group \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(k)\) is the group of all central division algebras over \(k\).

By Wedderburnâ€™s decomposition thereom, we know that if \(A\) is a central simple algebra over \(k\) \[\begin{equation} A \simeq M_n(D), \end{equation}\] for some division ring \(D\) over \(k\). To construct the Brauer group \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(k)\), we take the collection of all central simple algebras over \(k\) and identify \[\begin{equation} M_{ n_1 }(D_1) \sim M_{n_2}(D_2) \Leftrightarrow D_1 \simeq D_2, \end{equation}\] that is if the underlying division algebras are the same, we consider the central simple algebras to be the same. The group operation is via tensoring. \[\begin{equation} [A_1] \cdot [A_2 ] = [A_1 \otimes A_2]. \end{equation}\] To see that \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(k)\) is a group, we observe that the identity element is \(k\) itself and for any division ring \(D\) central over \(k\) \[\begin{equation} D\otimes D_{\mathop{\mathrm{opp}}} \simeq M_{r}(k), \end{equation}\] where \(r = {\dim_{k} D}\) and \(D_{\mathop{\mathrm{opp}}}\) is the opposite division algebra of \(D\). The map \(a \otimes b \mapsto ( x \mapsto axb )\) is the above isomorpism.

Examples

  • \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(\mathbb{Q}_p) \simeq \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}\).
  • \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(\mathbb{R}) \simeq \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}\). The only central division algebra over \(\mathbb{R}\) other that itself is \(\mathbb{H}\).
  • \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(\mathbb{F}_q) = 0\) for any finite field \(\mathbb{F}_q\) since there are no non-trivial division rings over them.

Galois cohomology

Let \(K/k\) be a Galois extenstion. Denote \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(K/k)\) as the kernel of the map \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(k) \rightarrow \mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(K)\) given by \([A] \mapsto [A \otimes_{k} K ]\). The kernel of this map are exactly central simple algebras over \(k\) that split over \(K\).

Then, we have an isomorphism \[\begin{equation} H^{2}(G,K^{*}) \simeq \mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(K/k). \end{equation}\]

Since we have that \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(k) = \bigcup_{K/k} \mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(K/k)\), in particular this implies that \(\mathop{\mathrm{Br}}(k)\) is a torsion group. See the notes here for details.
