\(\DeclareMathOperator{\SL}{SL}\) \(\DeclareMathOperator{\GL}{GL}\) For in-depth understanding, the reader may refer to [1] or [2].

A linear algebraic group is a subgroup \(\mathcal{G}\) of \(\SL_n(\mathbb{C})\) for some \(n \ge 1\) that is given as a zero set of some polynomials in the matrix entries \(X_{ij}: \SL_n(\mathbb{C}) \rightarrow \mathbb{C}\).

We say that \(\mathcal{G}\) is an algebraic \(k\)-group for a subfield \(k \subseteq \mathbb{C}\) if the ideal of the vanishing polynomials that define \(\mathcal{G}\) has generators in the ring \(k[{ X_{ij}]}_{1 \le i ,j \le n}\).

For a subring \(R \subseteq \mathbb{C}\), we denote \(\mathcal{G}(R)\) to be the set of solutions of the polynomials in \(\SL_{n}(R)\). We call \(\mathcal{G}(R)\) to be the \(R\)-points of \(\mathcal{G}\).

The actual definition is slightly subtler. For an in-depth discussion on these subtleties, see [3].

The group \(\GL_n(\mathbb{C})\) can be embedded in \(\SL_{n+1}(\mathbb{C})\) as per the following map: \[\begin{align} g \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} g & 0_{n \times 1} \\ 0_{1 \times n} & \det(g)^{-1} \end{bmatrix}. \end{align}\] The image can easily be described as the zero set of some polynomials. Hence, it is an example of an algebraic group. In this sense, the group \(\GL_n(\mathbb{R})\) and \(\GL_n(\mathbb{Z})\) hold the obvious meanings as above.

So, in this framework, whenever we write \(\GL_n(\mathbb{C})\) as an algebraic group, we secretly mean that it is embedded in \(\SL_{n+1}(\mathbb{C})\). In more technical terms, this gives \(\GL_n\) a structure of an affine algebraic variety and this is the algebraic structure that we care about on \(\GL_n\).

Every finite group \(G\) can be seen as an algebraic group.

Since \(\SL_n(\mathbb{C})\) and \(\SL_n(\mathbb{R})\) are Lie groups, and closed subgroups of Lie groups are also Lie groups (according to the classically known closed subgroup theorem, see [4]). In general, this is also true for \(\mathcal{G}(\mathbb{R})\) and \(\mathcal{G}(\mathbb{C})\) for an algebraic group \(\mathcal{G}\).

Morphisms

We also have a notion of algebraic morphisms.

For algebraic groups \(\mathcal{G}\) and \(\mathcal{H}\), a group homomorphism \(f:\mathcal{G} \rightarrow \mathcal{H}\) is a morphism of algebraic groups if the individual matrix entries of \(f(X) \in \mathcal{H}\) can be expressed as functions that are \(\mathbb{C}\)-polynomials in the entries \((X_{ij})_{1\le i, j \le r}\) of \(X\). If those polynomials can actually be allowed to have coefficients in a subring \(R \subseteq \mathbb{C}\), then the morphism \(f\) will be called an \(R\)-morphism.

An \(R\)-morphism from \(\mathcal{G} \rightarrow GL_d(\mathbb{C})\) is called a \(d\)-dimensional \(R\)-representation. \(1\)-dimensional \(R\)-representations are called \(R\)-characters.

Let us present an interesting lemma that could aid the reader in putting these concepts together.

Suppose \(\mathcal{G}\) is an algebraic group and let there be an arbitrary \(\mathbb{Q}\)-character \(\chi:\mathcal{G}\rightarrow \GL_1(\mathbb{C})\). Then \(\chi(\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} )) \subseteq \{ -1,1\}\). Furthermore, if \(\mathcal{G}(\mathbb{R})\) is connected as a Lie group, then \(\chi(\mathcal{G}(\mathbb{Z})) = \{ 1\}.\)

Clearly, image of \(\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} )\) under \(\chi\) will be a subgroup of \(\GL_1(\mathbb{Q})\). Now, we identify $_1() = { , t } $. Observe that since \(\chi\) is a \(\mathbb{Q}\)-morphism, \(t(X)\) can be expressed as a \(\mathbb{Q}\)-polynomial in the entries of \(X \in \mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} )\) which are integers. The coefficients of these polynomials are rational and will have a least common denominator \(N\) and therefore \(t \in \frac{1}{N} \mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{Q}^{*}\). However, the only multiplicatively closed subgroups of \(\frac{1}{N} \mathbb{Z}\) are \(\{ -1,1\}\) and \(\{ 1\}\).

For the latter statement, observe that \(\mathbb{Q}\)-morphisms are continuous functions and therefore, take connected sets to connected sets. Hence, \(\chi(G_{\mathbb{R}})\) is a connected subset of \(\GL_1(\mathbb{R})\) and must be entirely contained in the identity component of \(\GL_{1}(\mathbb{R})\). This \(\chi(g) \neq -1\) for any \(g \in \mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} )\).

The group of all \(\mathbb{Q}\)-characters of an algebraic group \(\mathcal{G}\) is denoted as \(X(\mathcal{G})\).

Borel-Harish Chandra theorem

See Borel-Harish Chandra for the main article.

Observe that both \(\SL_d(\mathbb{Z})\) and \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})\) are unimodular groups. For \(\SL_d(\mathbb{Z})\), this follows since it is discrete and for \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})\), this is a consequence of the fact that the modular function is a homomorphism \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R}) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{*}\) which must be trivial since \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})\) can be generated by commutators in the group.

The following is a classical theorem, due to Siegel [5].

With respect to any left \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})\)-invariant Haar measure on \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})/\SL_d\left( \mathbb{Z}\right)\), the volume of \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})/\SL_d(\mathbb{Z})\) is finite.

The fact that \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})/\SL_d(\mathbb{Z})\) has a finite volume makes it a probability space up to rescaling of the measure appropriately. In analogy with the finiteness of the Haar measure on \(\SL_d(\mathbb{R})/\SL_d(\mathbb{Z})\), the question we would like to ask is the following. As we will later see, answering this question will be important while addressing the existence of random lattices.

For a given \(\mathbb{Q}\)-algebraic group \(\mathcal{G} \subseteq \SL_d(\mathbb{C})\), does the quotient space \(\mathcal{G}(\mathbb{R})/\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} )\) admit a finite left \(\mathcal{G}(\mathbb{R})\)-invariant measure?

Here is an obstruction that must be navigated while attempting this question.

Suppose \(\chi: \mathcal{G}\rightarrow \GL_1(\mathbb{C})\) is a \(\mathbb{Q}\)-character such that \(\chi|_{\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{R} )}:\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{R} ) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{*}\) takes non-trivial values in a neighbourhood of identity. Let \(\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{R} )^{0} \subset \mathcal{G}( \mathbb{R} )\) be the identity component of the Lie group \(\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{R} )\) and \(\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} )^{0} = \mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} ) \cap \mathcal{G}( \mathbb{R} )^{0}\). Then \(\mathcal{G}({\mathbb{R}})^{0}/\mathcal{G}({\mathbb{Z}})^{0}\) cannot have a finite volume with respect to the Haar measure on \(\mathcal{G}_{0}\).

Suppose that there is a finite Haar measure \(\mu\) on \(\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{R} )^{0}/\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} )^{0}\). By , we know that \(\chi : \mathcal{G}( \mathbb{R} )^{0} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{*}\) descends to become a continuous surjective function \(\chi: \mathcal{G}( \mathbb{R} )^{0}/ \mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} )^{0} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{*}\). This surjective function can be used to push a Borel measure on \(\mathbb{R}^{*}\) by taking the measure of a Borel set \(E \subset \mathbb{R}^{*}\) to be \(\mu(\chi^{-1}(E))\). This gives us a finite measure on \(\mathbb{R}^{*}\). However, by the left-invariance of \(\mu\), the measure induced must be left-invariant under multiplication in \(\mathbb{R}^{*}\) (since \(\chi\) is surjective). This means that the usual Haar measure on \(\mathbb{R}^{*}\) equips it with the structure of a finite topological measure group, which is clearly false.

What Borel and Harish-Chandra proved in their 1962 work is that morally speaking, this is the only obstruction. The precise version of their statements is the following.

[6]

Let \(\mathcal{G}\) be an algebraic group defined over \(\mathbb{Q}\). Then \(\mathcal{G}(\mathbb{R})/\mathcal{G}( \mathbb{Z} )\) has a finite invariant measure if and only if \(X(\mathcal{G}^{0}) = \{ 1\}\), where \(\mathcal{G}^{0} \subseteq \mathcal{G}\) is the connected component of identity in the Zariski topology.

As a subgroup of \(\SL_{r+1}(\mathbb{C})\), the Zariski connected components and the usual connected components are the same for \(\mathcal{G}\).
