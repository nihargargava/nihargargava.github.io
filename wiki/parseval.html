Consider a lattice \(\Lambda \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{d}\). Let \(\Lambda^{*}\) be the dual lattice defined as \[\begin{align} \Lambda^{*} = \{ \lambda \in \mathbb{R}^{d}, \ | \ \langle \lambda,\Lambda\rangle \subseteq \mathbb{Z} \}. \end{align}\]

Define the following Fourier transform. \[\begin{align} \mathcal{F}:L^{2}(\mathbb{R}^{d}/ \Lambda) \rightarrow & L^{2}(\Lambda^{*}) \\ f \mapsto & \hat{f}(\lambda) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^{d} / \Lambda} f(x)e^{- 2\pi i \langle \lambda, x\rangle} dx \\ \end{align}\]

Then, the following is called Parseval identity, for \(f \in L^{2}(\mathbb{R}^{d} / \Lambda)\) and \(\hat{f}=\mathcal{F}(f)\). \[\begin{align} \int_{\mathbb{R}^{d}/\Lambda} |f(x)|^{2} dx = \sum_{\lambda \in \Lambda^{*}} | f(\lambda)|^{2}. \end{align}\]

This is actually a special case of the fact that \(\mathcal{F}\) induces an isometry of Hilbert spaces. It is also a special case of a more general identity in Pontryagin duality.
